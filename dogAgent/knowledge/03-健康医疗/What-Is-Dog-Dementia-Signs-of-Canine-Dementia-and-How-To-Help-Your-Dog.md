# What Is Dog Dementia? Signs of Canine Dementia and How To Help Your Dog

While senior dogs can live good, enjoyable lives with their pet parents, getting older is not without challenges. Dogs can develop dementia, also called cognitive dysfunction, as they age.

### Key Takeaways

  * Dog dementia is like Alzheimer’s disease in people. Affected dogs may act confused and forgetful. Dogs with dementia have age-related damage to their brain.
  * There is no cure for dog dementia. Cognitive dysfunction is managed rather than cured.
  * While dogs don’t necessarily recover from dementia, they can show improvement with nutritional, medical, and behavioral support.



## What Is Dog Dementia?

Dog dementia is like [Alzheimer’s disease](https://www.alz.org/alzheimers-dementia/what-is-alzheimers) in people. Affected dogs may act confused and forgetful. Dogs with dementia have age-related damage to their brain. These changes make them easily lost and [anxious](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/behavioral/dog-anxiety) and forget things that they used to know. Dog dementia doesn’t happen overnight. Cognitive dysfunction is usually a very gradual, slowly worsening syndrome.

Affected dogs often wander the house aimlessly, sometimes forgetting where they are. This is usually worse in the evening before bed. They may get lost in corners and [bark](https://www.petmd.com/dog/behavior/reasons-your-dog-wont-stop-barking) or generally seem more anxious.

Dog dementia is not considered a medical emergency.

## Symptoms of Dog Dementia

Common symptoms of dog dementia include:

  * Wandering aimlessly, especially at night



  * Getting stuck in corners or lost in the house



  * Barking or [howling](https://www.petmd.com/dog/behavior/why-do-dogs-howl), seemingly at nothing



  * Overall increase in anxiety



  * [Panting](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/why-do-dogs-pant) or looking stressed for no obvious reason



  * Forgetting where [food and water bowls](https://www.chewy.com/b/dog-feeding-supplies-and-accessories-338?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=food%20and%20water%20bowls&utm_content=/dog/conditions/neurological/signs-of-dog-dementia) are



  * [House soiling](https://www.petmd.com/dog/behavior/dog-pooping-house-why-and-what-do)



  * Changes in behavior and interactions with members of the household (avoiding social interactions with the rest of the family, becoming [irritable](https://www.petmd.com/dog/behavior/aggression-in-dogs), [clingy](https://www.petmd.com/dog/behavior/how-manage-clingy-dog-behavior-0), or [anxious about separation](http://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/behavioral/separation-anxiety-dogs))



  * Forgetting [tricks or previously known cues](https://www.petmd.com/dog/behavior)



## Causes of Dog Dementia

Dog dementia is caused by deterioration of the brain in aging dogs.

Dogs affected by cognitive dysfunction have a buildup of a protein called beta-amyloid that forms plaques on the brain.

These plaques slow the sending of signals between brain cells. These brain cells, called neurons, become dysfunctional, interfering with memory, learning, and spatial awareness. With damaged neurons and reduced blood flow to the brain, the mind does not work as well as it used to.

[Senior dogs](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/when-is-a-dog-considered-senior) are at the highest risk for developing this condition: 28% of dogs aged 11–12 are affected, and 68% of dogs 15–16 years old have cognitive dysfunction syndrome, according to [a study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11394831/) done on prevalence of dementia.

## How Veterinarians Diagnose Dog Dementia

A diagnosis of cognitive dysfunction syndrome is based on a history of your dog’s behaviors at home. Your veterinarian will likely want to rule out other medical conditions that can cause similar symptoms first before diagnosing dog dementia.

A [physical exam](https://www.petmd.com/general-health/pet-wellness-exams-how-prepare), [blood work](https://www.petmd.com/dog/procedure/blood-work-for-dogs-and-cats), and [X-rays](https://www.petmd.com/dog/procedure/x-rays-for-dogs) are often done in senior patients to screen for underlying disease. Many common problems in seniors can cause symptoms like dementia.

[Liver disease](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/digestive/liver-disease-dogs) can lead to ammonia buildup and mental confusion. [Kidney disease](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/urinary/chronic-kidney-disease-ckd-dogs), [urinary infections](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/urinary/uti-dogs-signs-causes-and-when-call-your-vet), and [bladder stones](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/urinary/bladder-stones-dogs-symptoms-prevention-and-more) can all lead to [potty accidents in the house](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/why-my-dog-peeing-house). [Arthritis](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/musculoskeletal/osteoarthritis-in-dogs) can lead to increased anxiety and pacing, as affected pets try to avoid the pain of laying down.

Rarely, [CT scans](https://www.petmd.com/dog/procedure/dog-ct-scan) may be recommended to look for any [tumors](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/cancer/brain-tumors-dogs) or [abnormalities in the brain](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/neurological/neurological-disorders-dogs).

Before your vet visit, sit down and think about your pet’s changes with age. You can ask yourself the following screening questions:

  * Has your dog been disoriented?



  * Have they had any changes in their interactions with others in the household?



  * Has their sleep cycle changed? For example, are they wandering the house at night?



  * Are they house soiling?



  * Do they have increased anxiety or unusual activity?



  * Has their learning ability changed?



If you answered yes to any of these questions, let your vet know. Your vet may ask you to fill out a [CAnine DEmentia Scale (CADES) worksheet](https://trimalaska.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/Canine-Dementia-Scale-CADES-worksheet-1.pdf) to check how severe their cognitive changes are.

## Treatment of Dog Dementia

There is no cure for dog dementia. Cognitive dysfunction is managed rather than cured.

The best thing you can do is catch dementia changes early and try to slow it down. Treatment is usually a combination of environmental changes, [nutritional supplements](https://www.petmd.com/dog/nutrition/what-are-dog-supplements-how-do-they-work), special diets, and medications.

Environmental modifications are important for pet patients with dog dementia. With changes to the brain as your dog ages, they may become confused and can accidentally hurt themselves.

Night lights can be helpful to minimize their confusion in the dark if they have night walks. Keeping your dog in a small room that is difficult to get lost in may reduce their vocalizing and confusion. Some senior pets may also be more comfortable in [a dog crate](https://www.chewy.com/b/crates-kennels-369?ref-query=dog crates&ref=searchRedirect&utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=a%20dog%20crate&utm_content=/dog/conditions/neurological/signs-of-dog-dementia) at night, such as this [Frisco Fold & Carry Double Door Collapsible Wire Dog Crate](https://www.chewy.com/frisco-heavy-duty-fold-carry-double/dp/116517?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=Frisco%20Fold%20%26%20Carry%20Double%20Door%20Collapsible%20Wire%20Dog%20Crate&utm_content=/dog/conditions/neurological/signs-of-dog-dementia).

##

Nutritional supplements can be helpful in slowing aging changes in the brain. Some studies have shown that [Omega-3 fatty acids](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/fish-oil-for-dogs), vitamin E, apoaequorin, and coenzyme Q supplements (like [Vetality](https://www.chewy.com/vetality-10in1-multi-well-bites-dog/dp/629430?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=Vetality&utm_content=/dog/conditions/neurological/signs-of-dog-dementia), Senilife, and Neutricks) may reduce dementia-related signs, while other studies have been inconclusive.

##

[Nutramax Denosyl](https://www.chewy.com/nutramax-denosyl-tablets-liver-brain/dp/108965?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=Nutramax%20Denosyl&utm_content=/dog/conditions/neurological/signs-of-dog-dementia) has a free radical scavenger, called [SAM-e](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/what-can-sam-e-do-dogs), that can help dogs with cognitive dysfunction have improved awareness and activity.

##

Diet therapy has been shown to be helpful for senior pets. Diets rich in antioxidants and medium chain triglycerides can aid brain function. [Hills Cognitive + Mobility Brain Care j/d](https://www.chewy.com/hills-prescription-diet-brain-care-jd/dp/1714742?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=Hills%20Cognitive%20%2B%20Mobility%20Brain%20Care%20j%2Fd&utm_content=/dog/conditions/neurological/signs-of-dog-dementia) and [Purina Neurocare](https://www.chewy.com/purina-pro-plan-veterinary-diets/dp/140162?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=Purina%20Neurocare&utm_content=/dog/conditions/neurological/signs-of-dog-dementia) are examples of [veterinary diets](https://www.petmd.com/dog/nutrition/veterinary-dog-food) specially made to help dogs with cognitive dysfunction.

##

Sometimes, vets will prescribe an [FDA](https://www.fda.gov/animal-veterinary)-approved medication called [selegiline](https://www.petmd.com/pet-medication/selegiline) to help with cognitive dysfunction and senility.

##

This medication can help dogs with dementia have fewer sleep-wake cycle issues, have less house soiling, and improve their brain activity.

**Selegiline can’t be given with certain medications, like[trazodone](https://www.petmd.com/pet-medication/trazodone-dogs), [Proin](https://www.petmd.com/pet-medication/proin), [fluoxetine](https://www.petmd.com/pet-medication/fluoxetine-reconcile-prozac-for-dogs), and [tramadol](https://www.petmd.com/pet-medication/tramadol-for-dogs-cats). **

Trazodone may also be prescribed by some veterinarians to help senior patients with dementia-related anxiety.

##

**When discussing medications with your veterinarian, make sure to let them know any meds that your dog is currently taking.**

## Recovery and Management of Dog Dementia

While dogs don’t necessarily recover from dementia, they can show improvement with nutritional, medical, and behavioral support.

The idea is to help keep the brain active and delay the speed that cognitive function worsens.

Realistic expectations for some pet parents may mean understanding that your dog’s life can look a little different as they enter [their golden years](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/how-to-care-for-senior-dog), but with patience and adaptability, quality of life can often be maintained.

Keeping a regular routine and schedule is helpful for pets with dementia. Increasing activity during the day, such as 30-minute [slow walks](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/dog-walking-tips-how-to-walk-your-dog) twice a day, can help the mind be more quiet at night.

##

[Treat puzzles](https://www.chewy.com/b/treat-toys-dispensers-2336?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=Treat%20puzzles&utm_content=/dog/conditions/neurological/signs-of-dog-dementia), such as [this DJ Doggo puzzle](https://www.chewy.com/brightkins-dj-doggo-puzzle-feeder-dog/dp/930054?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=this%20DJ%20Doggo%20puzzle&utm_content=/dog/conditions/neurological/signs-of-dog-dementia), can act like human crossword puzzles to exercise the mind.

##

Adding good traction with no-slip floor runners (like [these Bungalow Flooring squares](https://www.chewy.com/bungalow-flooring-squares-indoor/dp/837030?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=these%20Bungalow%20Flooring%20squares&utm_content=/dog/conditions/neurological/signs-of-dog-dementia)) or toe grips (like [these Dr. Buzby's Toe Grips](https://www.chewy.com/dr-buzbys-toegrips-dog-nail-grips-20/dp/1087726?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=these%20Dr.%20Buzby's%20Toe%20Grips&utm_content=/dog/conditions/neurological/signs-of-dog-dementia)) can help make movement easier, helping reduce your senior dog’s anxiety when they need to get around.

##

Other management strategies may be aimed at lowering your dog’s anxiety as they go through dementia changes. Plug-in wall pheromones, like [this one by ThunderEase](https://www.chewy.com/thunderease-calming-diffuser-dogs-30/dp/159744?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=this%20one%20by%20ThunderEase&utm_content=/dog/conditions/neurological/signs-of-dog-dementia), and calming collars, such as [this Zenidog collar,](https://www.chewy.com/zenidog-long-acting-calming-collar/dp/1760046?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=this%20Zenidog%20collar%2C&utm_content=/dog/conditions/neurological/signs-of-dog-dementia) are made with the pheromones released when puppies nurse from their mothers and may subtly reduce stress in some pups.

##

[ThunderShirts](https://www.chewy.com/thundershirt-classic-anxiety-calming/dp/41099?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=ThunderShirts&utm_content=/dog/conditions/neurological/signs-of-dog-dementia) can provide constant, gentle pressure to help relax some dogs.

##

Most pets with cognitive changes benefit from a combination of management options in their routine, environment, and diet, rather than just one thing.

## Prevention of Dog Dementia

Despite dementia being very common in dogs, it can be delayed by starting nutritional and behavioral support early on.

Keeping your senior dog on a good supplement, giving a senior diet, and maintaining a regular schedule with twice-daily walks will go a long way to help brain health.

## Dog Dementia FAQs

### What is the life expectancy of a dog with dementia?

As most dogs with dementia are already seniors at the time of diagnosis, the life expectancy is usually only a few years.

### What are the symptoms of dementia in dogs?

The most common symptoms of dementia in dogs are pacing at night, getting lost and vocalizing randomly, house soiling, increased anxiety, and behavior changes in their interaction with household members.

### How does a dog act if they have dementia?

Dogs with dementia generally act confused. They readily get lost and seem to forget things easily. They may go outdoors to go to the bathroom, forget to go, then go in the house. They may also get lost in corners, or you may find them in a room barking at seemingly nothing. They will often be restless in the evening and pace.

### Can a dog recover from dementia?

While dogs can’t recover from dementia, they can show improved symptoms, and you can slow the worsening of cognitive dysfunction.

### Why is dog dementia worse at night?

The sleep-wake cycle is controlled by a certain part of the brain.

When dogs get dementia they have a weakening of the brain, and this portion can be affected. Without good sleep-wake patterns, the internal clock is off, leading to confusion at night.
