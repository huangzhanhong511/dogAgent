# UTI in Dogs: Signs, Causes, and Treatment

[](/author/dr-michael-kearley)

Is your dog squatting to pee repeatedly and only getting little bits of urine out at a time?

If so, they may be suffering from a UTI (urinary tract infection).

Chronic licking of the vulva, blood in the urine, and increased thirst are other common dog UTI symptoms.

Urinary tract infections in dogs are common, especially in senior dogs and dogs that have a recessed vulva. Pups will usually respond well to antibiotics with prompt treatment.

In this article, you’ll everything you need to know about UTIs in dogs and how to help your pet.

### Key Takeaways

  * Urinary tract infections (UTIs) in dogs are common and typically caused by bacteria traveling up the urethra into the bladder.
  * Common signs include frequent urination, straining or pain while urinating, small amounts of urine, and sometimes blood or strong odor in the urine.
  * Prompt veterinary care is important. Untreated UTIs may lead to serious complications, and effective treatment usually involves appropriate antibiotics and follow-up.



## What Are Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs) in Dogs?

A urinary tract infection (UTI) in dogs is a common problem in veterinary medicine, with about 14% of dogs being affected.

UTIs are often caused by bacteria that are typically found in or around the rectum, skin, or hair near the external urethral opening traveling through the urethra into the bladder.

Less commonly, they can be [spread through the bloodstream](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/systemic/sepsis-dogs). Viral and [fungal diseases](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/identifying-and-treating-fungal-infections-dogs) can also cause UTIs in dogs, but they are less common.

A UTI in dogs [causes pain](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/how-to-tell-if-your-dog-is-in-pain) and swelling of the bladder, leading to symptoms like painful or [bloody urination](https://www.petmd.com/dog/symptoms/why-is-my-dog-peeing-blood).

### Types of UTIs in Dogs

A UTI in dogs can be either:

  1. **Simple or uncomplicated (sporadic cystitis)**. No predisposing factors or other diseases present; less than three UTIs in the past 12 months



  2. **Complicated or recurrent**. Seen in pets with underlying medical conditions or predisposing causes; pets who have had recent antibiotic usage; pets with more than three UTIs in the past 12 months.



**A UTI in a dog can be a veterinary emergency.**

If left untreated, they can cause life-threatening complications including pyelonephritis ([kidney infection](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/urinary/kidney-infections-dogs)), sepsis, and [kidney failure](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/urinary/kidney-failure-dogs).

## Symptoms of UTIs in Dogs

Symptoms of a UTI in dogs include:

  * [Straining to urinate](https://www.petmd.com/dog/symptoms/dog-not-peeing)



  * Painful urination (dysuria)



  * Passage of small amounts of urine, sometimes seen as dribbling



  * [Frequent urination](https://www.petmd.com/dog/symptoms/why-my-dog-peeing-lot)



  * [Urinary accidents](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/why-my-dog-peeing-house)



  * Licking genital region or inflammation (swelling) of the genital region, sometimes with salivary staining (copper coloring to the fur from spit)



  * Bloody urination



  * [Genital discharge](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/reproductive/vaginal-discharge-dogs)



  * Increased odor of urine



  * [Increased thirst](https://www.petmd.com/dog/symptoms/why-is-my-dog-drinking-a-lot-of-water)



Left untreated or in severe cases, more symptoms can occur. These include:

  * [Lethargy](https://www.petmd.com/dog/symptoms/why-my-dog-lethargic)



  * [Fever](https://www.petmd.com/dog/care/why-do-dogs-get-fevers)



  * [Decreased appetite](https://www.petmd.com/dog/symptoms/why-my-dog-not-eating)



## Causes of UTIs in Dogs

Most UTIs in dogs are caused by bacteria usually found on the skin or rectum and moving up the external urethral opening. Viral and fungal causes are less common.

The most common bacteria to cause infections include:

  * [E. coli](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/infectious-parasitic/e-coli-dogs)



  * _[Staphylococcus](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/skin/staph-infection-dogs) _



  * _[Streptococcus](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/infectious-parasitic/bacterial-infection-streptococcus-dogs) _



  * _Enterococcus_



  * _Enterobacter_



  * _Proteus spp._



### Risk Factors That Increase UTI Likelihood

Female dogs get UTIs more often than male pups, but any age and breed can be affected.

Middle-aged to older dogs, immunocompromised dogs, and dogs with certain comorbidities ([Cushing’s disease](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/endocrine/cushings-disease-in-dogs-symptoms-diagnosis), [diabetes mellitus](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/endocrine/diabetes-dogs), [kidney disease](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/urinary/chronic-kidney-disease-ckd-dogs)) are at higher risk.

Certain breeds are more prone to [developing crystals and bladder stones](http://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/bladder-stones-dogs-can-you-prevent-them) are at a higher risk of developing UTIs.

These include:

  * [Schnauzers](https://www.petmd.com/dog/breeds/standard-schnauzer)



  * [Dalmatians](https://www.petmd.com/dog/breeds/dalmatian)



  * [Yorkshire Terrier](https://www.petmd.com/dog/breeds/yorkshire-terrier)



  * [Shih Tzu](https://www.petmd.com/dog/breeds/shih-tzu)



  * [Poodles](https://www.petmd.com/dog/breeds/c_dg_poodle)



### Health Conditions That Can Cause UTIs

Certain health conditions can cause a UTI in dogs. These include:

  * [Neurologic disease](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/neurological/neurological-disorders-dogs) such as [IVDD](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/neurological/c_dg_intervertebral_disc_disease), [paralysis](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/neurological/paralysis-dogs), or brain or spinal cord trauma



  * [Urinary incontinence](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/incontinence-senior-dogs-what-do-and-how-help)



  * Ectopic ureters (congenital condition present at birth), more commonly seen in retrievers and [Entlebucher Mountain Dogs](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/rare-dog-breeds)



  * [Hooded or recessed vulvas](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/reproductive/recessed-vulva-dogs) in females



  * [Bladder cancer](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/cancer/bladder-cancer-dogs), often seen in [Scottish Terriers](https://www.petmd.com/dog/breeds/scottish-terrier)



  * [Polyps](https://www.petmd.com/dog/symptoms/lumps-bumps-and-cysts-dogs)



## How Veterinarians Diagnose UTIs in Dogs

To diagnose a UTI in dogs, vets will:

  * Perform [a thorough physical examination](https://www.petmd.com/general-health/pet-wellness-exams-how-prepare)



  * Check a dog’s urinary tract and genitalia looking for physical signs of disease or inflammation



  * Palpate (touch) the abdomen to check if the bladder is painful



  * Complete [a urinalysis](https://www.petmd.com/procedure/urinalysis-for-cats-and-dogs) to have a sample of your dog’s urine tested for evidence of infection.



  * Recommend sending the urine off for culture to determine what bacteria is growing and how best to treat it.



  * Possibly perform a rectal exam to palpate the distal urethra and prostate in male dogs



  * Perform a musculoskeletal and neurologic exam to check for neurologic conditions that make it challenging for a dog to urinate properly, or [signs of arthritis](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/musculoskeletal/osteoarthritis-in-dogs) that could affect how a dog urinates



  * [Perform blood work](https://www.petmd.com/dog/procedure/blood-work-for-dogs-and-cats)



  * Perform imaging such as [radiographs (X-rays)](https://www.petmd.com/dog/procedure/x-rays-for-dogs), [ultrasound](https://www.petmd.com/dog/procedure/dog-ultrasounds), or even cystoscopy (scoping of the urinary tract)



In advanced or refractory cases, [biopsy of the bladder](https://www.petmd.com/dog/procedure/cat-and-dog-biopsy) may be done, and, in males, a prostatic wash or prostate cytology may be recommended.

## Treatment of UTIs in Dogs

To treat a UTI in dogs, vets will prescribe a broad-spectrum antibiotic to be given for several days. Common antibiotics given include:

  * [Amoxicillin](https://www.petmd.com/pet-medication/amoxicillin-for-dogs-cats)



##

  * [Clavamox](https://www.petmd.com/pet-medication-clavamox-dogs-cats)



##

  * [TMS](https://www.petmd.com/pet-medication/smz-tmp-for-dogs) (trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole)



##

If the case is complicated, antibiotic treatment is extended out for several weeks while the underlying or predisposing cause is also treated.

If the UTI is complicated by the presence of bladder stones or crystals, treatment for a UTI in dogs could include surgery or a dissolution diet (such as [Royal Canin s/o](https://www.chewy.com/royal-canin-veterinary-diet-adult/dp/1216510?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=Royal+Canin%C2%AE+s%2Fo&utm_content=/dog/conditions/urinary/uti-dogs-signs-causes-and-when-call-your-vet) or Hill’s s/d), which helps the body dissolve certain kinds of bladder stones or crystals.

##

Surgery or [chemotherapy](https://www.petmd.com/dog/procedure/chemotherapy-for-dogs) may be needed to remove a polyp or cancerous mass or to correct an ectopic ureter. For dogs with underlying health conditions such as diabetes mellitus or Cushing’s, treatment of the UTI may also include better management of these diseases.

To help manage the discomfort and inflammation caused by a UTI in dogs, your vet may also prescribe pain medications such as:

  * [Gabapentin](https://www.petmd.com/pet-medication/gabapentin)



##

  * [Tramadol](https://www.petmd.com/pet-medication/tramadol-for-dogs-cats)



  * [NSAIDs](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/nsaids-dogs-everything-you-need-know) like [Rimadyl](https://www.petmd.com/pet-medication/rimadyl-carprofen)



## Recovery and Management of UTIs in Dogs

Symptoms of a UTI in dogs often start to get better after a few doses of the right antibiotic.

**Even if your dog’s condition has improved, it’s important to finish the entire course of the prescribed antibiotic treatment.**

It may be recommended to recheck your dog’s urine after a few weeks to make sure the infection is gone.

Dogs rarely need hospitalization for this condition.

For dogs with complicated UTIs, your veterinarian may recommend repeating the urine culture during treatment, as well as a week or two after finishing the antibiotics, to make sure the infection is gone.

[A diet change](https://www.petmd.com/dog/nutrition/how-to-switch-your-dogs-food) may have also been recommended for your pup, in which case it is very important to stick to this new diet after a gradual transition period.

Also, if you [encourage drinking](https://www.chewy.com/education/dog/health-and-wellness/how-to-get-a-dog-to-drink-water?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=encourage%20drinking&utm_content=/dog/conditions/urinary/uti-dogs-signs-causes-and-when-call-your-vet) and increase your dog’s water intake, your pup will likely urinate more, which lowers the chances for crystals to stay and grow into stones.

##

Canned [veterinary diets](https://www.petmd.com/hub/veterinary-diets-dogs-and-cats), like [Hill’s c/d urinary care](https://www.chewy.com/hills-prescription-diet-cd-multicare/dp/1804702?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=Hill%E2%80%99s%20c%2Fd%C2%A0urinary%20care&utm_content=/dog/conditions/urinary/uti-dogs-signs-causes-and-when-call-your-vet), can be helpful as they contain more water.

## Prevention of UTIs in Dogs

Unfortunately, not all causes of a UTI in dogs can be prevented. There are, however, a few recommendations that can decrease the risk of your dog developing one, including:

  * For male dogs, proper grooming of the preputial hair can reduce skin bacteria; the same is true for keeping [the vulvar region in females](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/dog-vagina-health-guide) cleaned.



  * [Females that are overweight](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/digestive/obesity-dogs) or have hooded vulvas are prone to UTIs, so [pet wipes](https://www.chewy.com/b/waterless-dog-grooming-supplies-357?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=pet%20wipes&utm_content=/dog/conditions/urinary/uti-dogs-signs-causes-and-when-call-your-vet) (such as these unscented [Earth Rated Thick Hypoallergenic Dog Grooming Wipes](https://www.chewy.com/earth-rated-dog-wipes-thick-plant/dp/355125?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=Females+that+are+overweight+or+have+hooded+vulvas+are+prone+to+UTIs%2C+so+pet+wipes+%28such+as%26nbsp%3Bthese+grooming+wipes&utm_content=/dog/conditions/urinary/uti-dogs-signs-causes-and-when-call-your-vet&_ga=2.112154883.621499865.1767007571-821230798.1754061843)) are a quick and handy tool that can be used to clean your dog after urination. Medicated wipes, like [Douxo S3 Pyo Antiseptic Antifungal Wipes](https://www.chewy.com/douxo-s3-pyo-antiseptic-antifungal/dp/290620?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=Douxo%20S3%20Pyo%20Antiseptic%20Antifungal%20Wipes&utm_content=/dog/conditions/urinary/uti-dogs-signs-causes-and-when-call-your-vet), may be recommended for dogs with recessed vulvas that tend to collect bacteria and yeast in their folds.



##

  * Speak to your veterinarian about the benefits of surgery to correct the hooded vulva and decrease your pup’s chance of getting a UTI.



  * Keeping track of how much your dog drinks, how often they pee, what it looks like ([color and odor and stream quality](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/what-your-pets-urine-says-about-their-health)), and [his posture](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/dog-peeing-positions-and-what-they-mean) can help you get veterinary care quickly if something changes.



  * Give your dog free access to water with frequent potty breaks. A water fountain may make him want to drink more.



##

  * If your pet is older, immunocompromised, or has an underlying disease, it’s important to have his urine checked regularly.

    * Urine strips, like [CheckUp Pet Wellness Urinalysis Testing Kit](https://www.chewy.com/checkup-pet-wellness-urinalysis/dp/283322?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=Urine+strips&utm_content=/dog/conditions/urinary/uti-dogs-signs-causes-and-when-call-your-vet), can be great for checking your dog’s urine at home and, if used over time, can show patterns in your dog’s health.



## UTIs in Dogs FAQs

### How can you tell if your dog has a UTI?

Most dogs with a UTI will show symptoms such as frequent urination, straining or painful urination, urinary accidents, or blood in their urine.

### Can a dog’s UTI resolve on its own?

Dogs diagnosed with a UTI that are symptomatic need treatment, which requires prescription antibiotics as well as other medications (like pain relievers), probiotics, or even a diet change.

Left untreated, it will progress and can lead to life-threatening conditions like pyelonephritis (kidney infection), sepsis, or kidney failure.

### How can I treat my dog’s urine infection at home?

[Probiotics](https://www.petmd.com/dog/vet-verified/best-probiotics-for-dogs) and [cranberry extract](https://www.petmd.com/dog/nutrition/can-dogs-eat-cranberries), like [Cranadin](https://www.chewy.com/nutramax-crananidin-cranberry-extract/dp/108975), may help fight UTIs in dogs; however, the evidence is lacking, and they may not always be successful.

Speak with your veterinarian if you are interested in these options, but UTIs are best treated by your veterinarian at the clinic.
