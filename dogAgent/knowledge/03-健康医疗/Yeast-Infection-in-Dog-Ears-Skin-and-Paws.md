# Yeast Infection in Dog Ears, Skin, and Paws

[](/author/dr-brittany-kleszynski)

Dog ear yeast infections and dog paw yeast infections are some of the most common reasons pet parents bring their dog to the veterinarian.

Incessant scratching and biting accompanied with moisture leads to the widely-reported [“Frito” smell](https://be.chewy.com/why-do-dogs-feet-smell-like-fritos/) that develops in your dog’s paws, ears or skin.

Read on to discover everything you need to know about how to manage these infections, some tips on how to prevent them, and how to keep your pup comfortable.

### Key Takeaways

  * Yeast are fungal organisms that live on a dog’s skin without causing problems.
  * When a dog’s immune system weakens or an underlying health condition is present, yeast can overgrow and cause an infection.
  * Signs of yeast infections in dogs can vary depending on what area of the body is affected.



## What Are Yeast Infections in Dogs?

Does your dog [frantically itch their skin](https://www.petmd.com/dog/symptoms/why-my-dog-itching-so-much)? Have you noticed a bad smell or greasy fur when you pet them?

If so, they may have a yeast infection.

Yeast are [fungal organisms](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/identifying-and-treating-fungal-infections-dogs) that normally live on a dog’s skin without causing problems. However, when a dog’s immune system weakens or an underlying health condition, such as [hypothyroidism](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/endocrine/c_dg_hypothyroidism), is present, yeast can overgrow and cause an infection.

> If a dog’s yeast infection is left untreated, it will progressively worsen and lead to extreme discomfort. Over time, the discomfort can cause the dog to become very sick.

The yeast that commonly causes skin infections in dogs is _Malassezia_ , which grows on warm, moist areas of the skin. Yeast infections cause extreme itchiness and changes to the skin, such as [thickening](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/skin/hyperkeratosis-dogs) and [discoloration](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/skin/hyperpigmentation-dogs) over time.

Yeast infections in dogs are common among pups of all ages.

While yeast infections in dogs are not considered medical emergencies, they should be treated by a veterinarian immediately to prevent symptoms from worsening. If left untreated, yeast infections can cause extreme discomfort, which can lead to [a decreased appetite](https://www.petmd.com/dog/symptoms/why-my-dog-not-eating) and [lethargy](https://www.petmd.com/dog/symptoms/why-my-dog-lethargic), necessitating prompt veterinary care.

Because yeast infections typically develop secondary to an underlying illness or suppressed immune system, they are not contagious among dogs or to pet parents.

### Types of Yeast Infections in Dogs

Yeast infections can affect various areas of the skin, including the ears, skin folds, paws, armpits, vulva, and groin.

  * **Ears** —[Dog ear yeast infections](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/dog-ear-infections)—also referred to as yeast otitis—cause a thick, brown discharge and strong odor in the ear canal. Affected dogs repeatedly scratch their ears to try to relieve the discomfort. They may also shake their heads and develop a head tilt. Ear infections are commonly related to food allergies.

    * They are also more common in dog breeds with long ears, such as [Cocker Spaniels](https://www.petmd.com/dog/breeds/cocker-spaniel), [Labrador Retrievers](https://www.petmd.com/dog/breeds/labrador-retriever), and [Basset Hounds](https://www.petmd.com/dog/breeds/basset-hound).

  * **Skin** —Yeast dermatitis refers to a yeast infection anywhere on the skin. Affected dogs have a musty odor, greasy coat, [flaky](https://www.petmd.com/dog/symptoms/dry-flaky-skin-dogs) or thickened skin, and [hair loss](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/skin/c_dg_alopecia). They tend to lick the affected areas of the body, which leads to brown discoloration of the skin over time.

    * [Dermatitis](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/skin/contact-dermatitis), which is often due to allergies or a suppressed immune system, can affect a dog’s skin folds, armpits, vulva, and groin.

  * **Paws** —Dog paw yeast infections are generally related to environmental allergies, such as [allergies to grass or pollen](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/food-allergies-vs-seasonal-allergies-dogs). Dogs constantly [chew or lick their itchy paws](https://www.petmd.com/dog/slideshows/10-common-paw-problems-dogs), which leads to brown discoloration between the paw pads. Over time, open sores can develop on the paws.



## Symptoms of Yeast Infections in Dogs

Signs of yeast infections in dogs can vary depending on what area of the body is affected.

Symptoms of**yeast otitis** may include:

  * [Shaking of the head](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/ears/head-shaking-dogs-when-worry)

  * Scratching of the ears

  * Thick, brown ear discharge

  * Red, inflamed ear canals

  * Strong odor



Symptoms of **yeast dermatitis** may include:

  * Itchy skin

  * [Small, red bumps on the skin](https://www.petmd.com/dog/symptoms/lumps-bumps-and-cysts-dogs)

  * Brown discoloration of the skin

  * Frequent licking or chewing of the affected area

  * Greasy hair coat

  * Thickened skin

  * Strong odor



Symptoms of **a yeast infection affecting the paws** may include:

  * Licking and chewing the paws

  * Brown discoloration of the skin and fur

  * Red, moist areas between the paw pads



## Causes of Yeast Infections in Dogs

Yeast is opportunistic, which means that although it is naturally present on the skin, it can cause an infection when conditions are favorable.

Yeast infections in dogs can develop if a pup has a weak immune system or an underlying health condition. Underlying environmental or [food allergies](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/digestive/food-allergies-dogs) can predispose dogs to developing yeast infections.

Skin parasites, including [fleas](https://www.petmd.com/dog/how-prevent-and-get-rid-fleas-and-ticks-dogs) and [mites](https://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/skin/c_dg_sarcoptic_mange), can cause dogs to repeatedly itch their skin, which disrupts the normal skin barrier, making an infection more likely.

Some breeds—such as [Pit Bulls](https://www.petmd.com/dog/breeds/american-pit-bull-terrier), [Boxers](https://www.petmd.com/dog/breeds/boxer), and Cocker Spaniels—are more frequently affected by yeast infections.

## How Veterinarians Diagnose Yeast Infections in Dogs

A vet may use several tests to diagnose a yeast infection in dogs.

First, they will gather a thorough medical history, including when the symptoms started, if your pet is taking any medications, and if they have previously been diagnosed with any health conditions.

Next, your veterinarian will perform [a full physical exam](https://www.petmd.com/general-health/pet-wellness-exams-how-prepare) to check your dog’s overall health. They may also perform [blood work](https://www.petmd.com/dog/procedure/blood-work-for-dogs-and-cats) to check for any abnormalities.

Additional tests will depend on what your veterinarian finds during the initial exam, but may include the following:

  * **Tape impression** —During this test, your vet presses a piece of clear tape to the affected area of skin to collect a sample. It’s then stained and examined under a microscope to identify yeast.

  * **Cytology** —A cotton swab is used to collect a sample from the affected area, and it is then rubbed gently onto a microscope slide. The sample is then stained and examined under a microscope to look for yeast.

  * **Culture** —A sterile cotton swab is used to collect a sample from the affected area. It is then sent to a lab to identify the type of organism present.

  * **Skin biopsy** —For chronic infections or those that are unresponsive to treatment, [a small sample of skin](https://www.petmd.com/dog/procedure/cat-and-dog-biopsy) can be taken and sent to a lab for a more accurate diagnosis.



## Treatment of Yeast Infections in Dogs

Treatment for yeast infections in dogs will depend on the location and severity of the infection. Yeast dermatitis is often treated with [antifungal medications](https://www.petmd.com/pet-medication/fluconazole) and [prescription shampoos](https://www.petmd.com/dog/grooming/do-you-need-medicated-dog-shampoo).

##

Most infections require a few weeks of treatment. Severe infections can often take several weeks or even months to completely clear.

If the infection affects only one area, a topical medication may be all that is needed. For more widespread infections, oral medications and [frequent bathing](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/how-often-should-you-bathe-your-dog) can help resolve the issue.

Yeast otitis is typically treated with topical antifungal medications and steroids to reduce inflammation. Your veterinarian may recommend that you use a [medicated ear wash](https://www.petmd.com/dog/vet-verified/best-ear-cleaners-dogs) prior to instilling ear medications.

Paw yeast infections may benefit from [medicated antifungal wipes](https://www.chewy.com/mal-ket-wipes-dogs-cats/dp/111417?utm_source=petmd.com&utm_medium=referral&utm_campaign=dog-conditions&utm_term=medicated+antifungal+wipes&utm_content=/dog/conditions/skin/yeast-infections-dogs) and oral medications, a topical mousse, including antifungals and [steroids](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/7-types-steroids-dogs).

## Recovery and Management of Yeast Infections in Dogs

Because yeast infections in dogs often develop secondary to another health condition, they will not resolve without veterinary treatment.

Determining the underlying cause of the yeast infection and treating it accordingly improves treatment success. For example, if your dog has an underlying food allergy, an accurate diagnosis and treatment can help their skin improve.

It can take a few weeks to a few months for a yeast infection to fully resolve, depending on the severity. Most dogs start to feel relief from their symptoms in a week or two, but it can take several additional weeks for a pet parent to notice significant improvement to their skin and coat.

## Prevention of Yeast Infections in Dogs

Ensuring your dog receives [regular preventive care](https://www.petmd.com/dog/general-health/how-often-should-i-take-my-dog-veterinarian) and [vaccinations](https://www.petmd.com/dog/care/dog-vaccinations-for-every-lifestage) keeps them healthy and less likely to develop infections.

Additionally, health conditions that can lead to yeast infections in dogs can be caught and managed early during routine wellness visits.

Yeast infections in dogs are a nuisance to manage and can lead to serious complications, not to mention discomfort from the incessant scratching and odor. Thankfully, dog yeast infections can be treated with medication. In certain cases, yeast infections are preventable.

Be sure to have your pup examined by the vet at the first sign of itching or chewing.

## Yeast Infections in Dogs FAQs

### What food causes yeast infections in dogs?

Most of the food ingredients that cause allergies in dogs are proteins. Chicken is the most common food allergen related to yeast infections.

### What happens if dog yeast infections go untreated?

If a dog’s yeast infection is left untreated, it will progressively worsen and lead to extreme discomfort. Over time, the discomfort can cause the dog to become very sick.

### How do I know if my dog has a bacterial or yeast infection?

Unfortunately, it’s not always easy to tell if your dog’s infection is caused by either bacteria or yeast. Both can cause similar symptoms and appearance in your dog, but yeast infections often lead to more skin thickening, a greasy haircoat, and more odor.

### What dog breeds are prone to yeast infections?

Any dog can be affected by yeast infections, but those that already suffer from allergies or are predisposed to ear infections, like Cocker Spaniels, are at increased risk.
