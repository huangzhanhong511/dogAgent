## What Is Syncope in Dogs?

Syncope in dogs is the medical term for fainting caused by a temporary lack of oxygen or nutrients to the brain. **It is considered a medical emergency—immediate treatment is urgent and critical.**

Syncope is more of a clinical sign than a disease. It involves a sudden, temporary loss of consciousness associated with collapse. Dogs usually recover within seconds to minutes.

### Key Takeaways

  * Syncope in dogs is a temporary loss of consciousness caused by reduced oxygen or blood flow to the brain.
  * Canine fainting is considered a medical emergency and requires immediate veterinary evaluation.
  * Heart disease is the most common underlying cause of syncope in dogs.
  * Treatment depends entirely on identifying and managing the underlying condition.



## Symptoms of Syncope in Dogs

The most obvious symptom of syncope in dogs is sudden collapse, often with stiff limbs and body.

Your dog may stagger or lean before falling to one side. Urination and, rarely, defecation may occur during the episode. Some dogs may have an underlying heart murmur or arrhythmia.

Most episodes are brief, and dogs recover quickly

### What Is the Difference Between a Seizure and Syncope?

Syncope and [seizures](https://www.petmd.com/dog/symptoms/seizures-in-dogs) can look similar, but they have different causes and recovery patterns.

Syncopal episodes typically:

  * Are brief in duration
  * May be triggered by excitement or coughing
  * Have rapid recovery
  * May occur in dogs with heart murmurs or arrhythmias



Seizures typically:

  * Involve abnormal brain activity
  * Include a pre-ictal phase (pacing, whining, acting unusual)
  * Cause twitching movements, especially facial
  * Have longer recovery with disorientation
  * May include salivation, urination, or defecation
  * May leave neurologic deficits afterward



A video of the event can be extremely helpful for diagnosis.

## Causes of Syncope in Dogs

Syncope in dogs occurs when blood flow or oxygen delivery to the brain is suddenly reduced.

Possible causes include:

Cardiovascular conditions

  * [Arrhythmias](http://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/cardiovascular/c_dg_premature_ventricular)
  * [Heart failure](http://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/cardiovascular/c_multi_congestive_heart_failure_rt)
  * [Dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM)](http://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/cardiovascular/c_dg_cardiomyopathy_dilated)
  * Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM)
  * Mitral valve disease
  * Pericardial effusion
  * Pulmonary hypertension
  * Congenital heart defects
  * [Heartworm disease](http://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/infectious-parasitic/c_dg_heartworm_disease)
  * Cancer affecting blood output



Neurologic conditions

  * [Brain tumors](http://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/cancer/c_multi_brain_tumors)
  * Vascular disease
  * Narcolepsy



Situational syncope

  * Coughing
  * Vomiting
  * Urination
  * Defecation
  * Tight collar or leash pressure



Vasovagal syncope

  * Sudden drop in blood pressure triggered by stress or emotion



Other causes

  * Acute hemorrhage (blood loss)
  * Profound [anemia](http://www.petmd.com/dog/conditions/systemic/anemia-dogs) (low red blood cell count)
  * Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar)
  * Electrolyte abnormalities
  * Drug side effects (vasodilators, beta blockers)



## How Veterinarians Diagnose Syncope in Dogs

Diagnosing syncope in dogs involves identifying the underlying cause through history, physical exam, and diagnostic testing.

A detailed description (or video) of the event is extremely helpful.

Diagnostic tests may include:

  * Bloodwork and urinalysis
  * Chest X-rays
  * Electrocardiogram (EKG)
  * 24-hour Holter monitor for arrhythmias
  * Blood pressure measurement
  * Echocardiogram



Referral to a cardiologist or neurologist may be recommended for CT, MRI, or CSF tap.

## Treatment for Syncope in Dogs

Treatment for syncope in dogs depends entirely on the underlying cause.

For situational syncope:

  * Avoid known triggers
  * Switch from collar to [harness](http://www.chewy.com/b/harnesses-347)
  * Modify environmental triggers (e.g., disconnect doorbell)



For heart conditions:

  * [Sotalol](http://www.chewy.com/sotalol-hcl-generic-tablets/dp/173730) (anti-arrhythmic)
  * [Enalapril](http://www.chewy.com/enalapril-maleate-generic-tablets/dp/173490) (blood pressure regulation)
  * Pacemaker placement
  * Surgery for tumors or pericardial effusion
  * [Chemotherapy](https://www.petmd.com/dog/procedure/chemotherapy-for-dogs) or radiation (to treat cancer)



For metabolic causes:

  * IV fluids
  * Glucose supplementation
  * Blood transfusion



Medication changes may also be necessary.

## Recovery and Management of Syncope in Dogs

Preventing future episodes requires identifying and managing the underlying cause.

Management may include:

  * Avoiding excitement triggers
  * Limiting strenuous activity
  * Installing safety gates
  * Blocking stair access
  * Padding furniture corners



Dogs on medication will often require lifelong therapy and regular monitoring, including Holter monitoring for arrhythmias.

## Syncope in Dogs FAQs

### What does fainting look like in dogs?

Syncope in dogs typically looks like sudden collapse with stiff legs, followed by rapid recovery within seconds to minutes. Urination may occur during the episode.

###  What do I do if my dog faints?

If your dog faints, remain calm, ensure they are safe from injury, and seek veterinary care immediately. Most dogs recover quickly, but fainting is a medical emergency.
